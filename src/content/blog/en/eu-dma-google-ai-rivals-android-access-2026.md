In late April 2026, Brussels returned to the spotlight for European product teams. Competition authorities continued **tightening Digital Markets Act (DMA) expectations** around behaviors that could privilege one deeply integrated assistant over rivals on **Android**.

You do not need to be a lawyer to grasp the practical point: **who controls system shortcuts** shapes how users discover apps, complete tasks by voice, and move data through privileged APIs.

## What founders and web leads should hear

**Audience ownership.** A strong website remains the channel you control when mobile defaults shift. If your growth relies on a single assistant integration, your funnel is fragile.

**Multi-provider as insurance.** Designing conversational hooks around **one actor** is convenient but brittle; DMA pressure could widen competitive surfaces.

**Compliance as leverage.** SMEs that document data flows and trim unnecessary SDKs suffer less when platforms churn.

## A quick review checklist

1. **Dependency map:** where do your apps or sites tie into OEM login, push, or proprietary widgets?
2. **Web-first experience:** speed, Core Web Vitals, and clear forms buffer turbulence in stores and assistants.
3. **Data contracts:** which subprocessors engage when users trigger OS-level AI features?

## Bottom line

The tug-of-war between **Google and EU regulators over rival AI access on Android** is a prompt to ask whether you truly own the customer relationship—or delegated too much to one gatekeeper. Companies investing in **owned web experiences, clear data practices, and flexible architecture** navigate these shifts better.
